Definition. Let [mathjaxinline]{\bf {F}}[/mathjaxinline] be a field. A function [mathjaxinline]T: {\bf {F}}^ n \rightarrow {\bf {F}}^ m[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation if both of the following properties hold:

  • For any vectors [mathjaxinline]\vecv , \vecw[/mathjaxinline] in the domain, [mathjaxinline]T(\vecv + \vecw ) = T(\vecv ) + T(\vecw ).[/mathjaxinline]

  • For any vector [mathjaxinline]\vecv[/mathjaxinline] in the domain, and any scalar [mathjaxinline]a\in {\bf {F}}[/mathjaxinline], [mathjaxinline]T(a\vecv ) = aT(\vecv )[/mathjaxinline].

Informally, if [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] satisfies the first condition we say that “[mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] respects vector addition", and if [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] satisfies the second condition we say that “[mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] respects scalar multiplication."

Definition. An elementary matrix is a square matrix of one of three types.

An elementary matrix of the first type is one obtained from the identity matrix by changing the diagonal entry in the [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th row and column to a number [mathjaxinline]c[/mathjaxinline], where [mathjaxinline]c \ne 0[/mathjaxinline]:
[mathjax]E_1 = \left[ \begin{array}{ccccccc} 1 & & & & & & \\ & \ddots & & & & & \\ & & 1& & & & \\ & & & c & & & \\ & & & & 1 & & \\ & & & & & \ddots & \\ & & & & & & 1 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]
Multiplying a matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] by [mathjaxinline]E_1[/mathjaxinline] (where [mathjaxinline]E_1[/mathjaxinline] is on the left) has the same effect as scaling row [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline] of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] by the scalar [mathjaxinline]c[/mathjaxinline].

An elementary matrix of the second type is one obtained from the identity matrix by changing the non-diagonal entry in the [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th row and [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th column ([mathjaxinline]i\ne j[/mathjaxinline]) to a number [mathjaxinline]c \ne 0[/mathjaxinline]:
[mathjax]E_2 = \left[ \begin{array}{ccccccc} 1 & & & & & & \\ & \ddots & & & & & \\ & & 1& & & & \\ & & & \ddots & & & \\ & & c & & 1 & & \\ & & & & & \ddots & \\ & & & & & & 1 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]
Multiplying a matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] by [mathjaxinline]E_2[/mathjaxinline] (where [mathjaxinline]E_2[/mathjaxinline] is on the left) has the same effect as adding [mathjaxinline]c[/mathjaxinline] times the [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th row of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] to row [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline].

An elementary matrix of the third type is one obtained from the identity matrix by changing the [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th and [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th diagonal entries to 0, and changing the entries in the [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th row and [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th column, and the [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th row and [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th column, to 1:

The last matrix operation we will introduce in this section is the transpose. The transpose of a matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] is essentially the matrix whose columns are the rows of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] (and whose rows are the columns of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline]). Essentially, [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] gets flipped across its diagonal. More formally:

Definition. Given an [mathjaxinline]m\times n[/mathjaxinline] matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline], the transpose of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] is the [mathjaxinline]n\times m[/mathjaxinline] matrix denoted [mathjaxinline]A^ t[/mathjaxinline] whose entry in the [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th row and [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th column is the same as the entry in the [mathjaxinline]j[/mathjaxinline]th row and [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th column of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline].

For instance, if

[mathjax]A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 3 & 4 & -1 \end{array} \right],[/mathjax]

then

[mathjax]A^ t = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \\ 0 & -1 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]
