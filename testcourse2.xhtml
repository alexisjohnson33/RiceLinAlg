At the end of this sequence, and after some practice, you should be able to:

  • Find the standard matrix for a linear transformation from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ n[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ m[/mathjaxinline].

  • Know what geometric properties are preserved by linear transformations from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ n[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ m[/mathjaxinline].

For time budgeting purposes, this sequence has 4 videos totaling 14 minutes, plus some questions.

Consider the following image.

When we apply the linear transformation [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^2 \rightarrow \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] given by [mathjaxinline]T(x) = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] to the image, which picture is the result?


Answer:

We see that [mathjaxinline]T\Bigg(\left[ \begin{array}{c} a\\ b\end{array} \right]\Bigg)=\left[ \begin{array}{cc} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{c} a\\ b\end{array}\right]=\left[ \begin{array}{c} -a\\ b\end{array}\right][/mathjaxinline] and thus [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] reflects the original image over the [mathjaxinline]y-[/mathjaxinline]axis.

Consider the following image.

When we apply the linear transformation [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^2 \rightarrow \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] given by [mathjaxinline]T(x) = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] to the image, which picture is the result?

Answer:

We see that

[mathjax]T\Bigg(\left[ \begin{array}{c} a\\ b\end{array} \right]\Bigg)=\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{c} a\\ b\end{array}\right]=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0.5a\\ 2b\end{array}\right],[/mathjax]

and thus scales the original image by a factor of 0.5 in the [mathjaxinline]x[/mathjaxinline] direction and a factor of 2 in the [mathjaxinline]y[/mathjaxinline] direction.

Suppose [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^4\rightarrow \mathbb R^3[/mathjaxinline] is given by [mathjaxinline]T(x) = Ax[/mathjaxinline] where

[mathjax]A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ 2 & 0 & -1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 & 3 & -2 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]

What is [mathjaxinline]T(e_3)[/mathjaxinline]?

To enter a vector such as [mathjaxinline]\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline], you can either:

  • enter it as you would a [mathjaxinline]3\times 1[/mathjaxinline] matrix: [[1],[2],[3]]

  • or, you can enter it as <1,2,3>

Use decimals only.

Answer:

Since [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^4\rightarrow \mathbb R^3[/mathjaxinline], we know from context that [mathjaxinline]e_3=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right].[/mathjaxinline] Then

[mathjax]T(e_3)=\left[ \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ 2 & 0 & -1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 & 3 & -2 \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 5 & -1 & 3 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]

Alternatively, if the four columns of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] are [mathjaxinline]v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4[/mathjaxinline], multiplying [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] by [mathjaxinline]\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] should yield the linear combination [mathjaxinline]0v_1 + 0v_2 + 1v_3 + 0v_4,[/mathjaxinline] which is just [mathjaxinline]v_3[/mathjaxinline].

Suppose the function [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^3\rightarrow \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] is given by

[mathjax]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2\\ a_3 \end{array} \right] \right) =\left[ \begin{array}{c} 2a_1-a_3 \\ -a_1 + a_2 \end{array} \right][/mathjax]

for all vectors [mathjaxinline]\left[ \begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2\\ a_3 \end{array} \right] \in \mathbb R^3[/mathjaxinline].

For what matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] is [mathjaxinline]T(x) = Ax[/mathjaxinline] for all [mathjaxinline]x \in \mathbb R^3[/mathjaxinline]?

To enter the matrix [mathjaxinline]\left[ \begin{array}{cc:c} 0& 1& 2 \\ 1& 2& 3 \end{array} \right],[/mathjaxinline] type [[0,1,2],[1,2,3]]

Use decimals only.

Is [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] a linear transformation?

Answer:

We see that

[mathjax]T\left[ \begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array} \right][/mathjax]

, so [mathjaxinline]A = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] works.

Since [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is multiplication by a matrix, it is a linear transformation.

For the next two problems, we will be considering the linear transformation [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^2 \rightarrow \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] given by rotation counterclockwise by [mathjaxinline]45^\circ[/mathjaxinline].

From the [mathjaxinline]45^\circ -45^\circ -90^\circ[/mathjaxinline] triangles in the diagram, we can find that [mathjaxinline]T(e_1) = \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right] \approx \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0.707 \\ 0.707 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] and [mathjaxinline]T(e_2) = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -\frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right] \approx \left[ \begin{array}{c} -0.707 \\ 0.707 \end{array} \right].[/mathjaxinline]

What is [mathjaxinline]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] \right)[/mathjaxinline]? (Remember to use decimals only, to within two decimal places.)

Answer:

Since [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation,

[mathjax]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] \right)=4 T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] \right)=4 T(e_1)=4\left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2\sqrt {2} \\ 2\sqrt {2} \end{array} \right]\approx \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2.83 \\ 2.83 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]

What is [mathjaxinline]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 1 \end{array} \right] \right)[/mathjaxinline]?

Answer:

Since [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation,

[mathjax]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 1 \end{array} \right] \right)=T(4e_1+e_2)=4T(e_1)+T(e_2)=4\cdot \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right]+\left[ \begin{array}{c} -\frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right] \approx \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2.12 \\ 3.54 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]

If [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is still rotation counterclockwise by [mathjaxinline]45^\circ[/mathjaxinline], what is [mathjaxinline]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3\sqrt {2} \\ -\sqrt {2} \end{array} \right] \right)[/mathjaxinline]?

Answer:

We could do this using linearity properties, but it is easier to just use the matrix [mathjaxinline]A = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] we found in the video. We know that [mathjaxinline]T(x) = Ax[/mathjaxinline] for all [mathjaxinline]x \in \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline], so

[mathjax]T\left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3\sqrt {2} \\ -\sqrt {2} \end{array} \right] \right) = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt {2}}{2} \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3\sqrt {2} \\ -\sqrt {2} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4\\ 2 \end{array} \right].[/mathjax]

Proposition/Definition. If [mathjaxinline]T: \mathbb R^ n \rightarrow \mathbb R^ m[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation, then there is a unique [mathjaxinline]m \times n[/mathjaxinline] matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] such that [mathjaxinline]T(x) = Ax[/mathjaxinline] for all [mathjaxinline]x\in \mathbb R^ n[/mathjaxinline]. This matrix [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] is called the standard matrix for [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline], and must be given by the formula

[mathjax]A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} | & | & & | \\ T(e_1) & T(e_2) & \cdots & T(e_ n) \\ | & | & & | \end{array} \right],[/mathjax]

where [mathjaxinline]e_ i \in \mathbb R^ n[/mathjaxinline] is the vector whose [mathjaxinline]i[/mathjaxinline]th entry equals 1, and all of whose other entries are 0.

True or false: Every linear transformation from a vector space [mathjaxinline]V[/mathjaxinline] to a vector space [mathjaxinline]W[/mathjaxinline] has a standard matrix.

Answer:

False. Only linear transformations from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ n[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^ m[/mathjaxinline] (or, more generally, from [mathjaxinline]F^ n[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]F^ m[/mathjaxinline] where [mathjaxinline]F[/mathjaxinline] is a field) have standard matrices.

For all [mathjaxinline]v\in \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline], let [mathjaxinline]T(v)[/mathjaxinline] be the reflection of [mathjaxinline]v[/mathjaxinline] across the horizontal axis of [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline]. [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline]. What is its standard matrix?

Answer:

If [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is a reflection across the horizontal axis, then [mathjaxinline]e_1[/mathjaxinline] is fixed by [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] and thus the first column of the standard matrix is [mathjaxinline]T(e_1)=e_1[/mathjaxinline]. Now, [mathjaxinline]e_2[/mathjaxinline] is reflected to [mathjaxinline]-e_2[/mathjaxinline], and thus the second column of the standard matrix is [mathjaxinline]T(e_2)=-e_2[/mathjaxinline]. So the answer is [mathjaxinline]\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1& 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{array} \right].[/mathjaxinline]

For all [mathjaxinline]v\in \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline], let [mathjaxinline]S(v)[/mathjaxinline] be the vector obtained by reflecting [mathjaxinline]v[/mathjaxinline] across the diagonal line [mathjaxinline]x=y[/mathjaxinline] and then rotating the result by [mathjaxinline]90^\circ[/mathjaxinline] counterclockwise. [mathjaxinline]S[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline]. What is its standard matrix?

Answer:

First, under the reflection, [mathjaxinline]e_1[/mathjaxinline] is taken to [mathjaxinline]e_2[/mathjaxinline]. Then, under the rotation, this vector is taken to [mathjaxinline]-e_1[/mathjaxinline]. Thus, [mathjaxinline]S(e_1)=-e_1[/mathjaxinline]; this will be the first column of the standard matrix. Similarly, [mathjaxinline]e_2[/mathjaxinline] is first taken to [mathjaxinline]e_1[/mathjaxinline] and then to [mathjaxinline]e_2[/mathjaxinline], and thus [mathjaxinline]S(e_2)=e_2[/mathjaxinline]; this is the second column. So the answer is [mathjaxinline]\left[ \begin{array}{cc} -1& 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right].[/mathjaxinline]

How many linear transformations [mathjaxinline]T:\mathbb R^4 \rightarrow \mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] satisfy the conditions [mathjaxinline]T(e_1) = T(e_3) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 3 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] and [mathjaxinline]T(e_4) = \mathbf{0}[/mathjaxinline]?

zero one more than one but finitely many infinitely many

Answer:

This question is equivalent to asking 'how many [mathjaxinline]2\times 4[/mathjaxinline] real matrices [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] satisfy [mathjaxinline]A e_1=A\cdot e_3=\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 3 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] and [mathjaxinline]Ae_4=\mathbf{0}[/mathjaxinline]?'. The first equality forces the first and third columns of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] to both be [mathjaxinline]\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 3 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline], and the second equality forces the fourth column of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] to be [mathjaxinline]\mathbf{0}[/mathjaxinline], but the third column of [mathjaxinline]A[/mathjaxinline] is free to have any real entries. Thus there are infinitely many choices.

Take a look at the video again. Which properties seem to be preserved by linear transformations from [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline] to [mathjaxinline]\mathbb R^2[/mathjaxinline]?

For instance, does every transformation preserve the area of regions?

Do lines that start parallel always go in the same direction after transformation?

Do lines that start perpendicular remain perpendicular after transformation?

Answer:

We see from the video that area is not preserved – the image can be much smaller or bigger after transformation than the original. Similarly, at time 2:12 in the video, we see an example of perpendicular lines not remaining perpendicular after a transformation.

However, parallel lines do always stay in the same direction, as seen in the video.

If you want to see a rigorous proof of this, click below.

Note that we can treat each point, [mathjaxinline](x,y)[/mathjaxinline], as a vector in standard position [mathjaxinline]\left[\begin{array}{c} x\\ y \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline]. Now, suppose [mathjaxinline]\ell _1[/mathjaxinline] and [mathjaxinline]\ell _2[/mathjaxinline] are two parallel lines. If [mathjaxinline]\ell _1[/mathjaxinline] consists of points [mathjaxinline]p_1=\left[\begin{array}{c} x\\ mx+b_1 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] then [mathjaxinline]\ell _2[/mathjaxinline] consists of points [mathjaxinline]p_2=\left[\begin{array}{c} x\\ mx+b_2 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline]. We will find the image of two points on each line and use these points to show the slope of the image lines, [mathjaxinline]T(\ell _ i)[/mathjaxinline], are the same. If [mathjaxinline]T[/mathjaxinline] is a linear transformation with standard matrix [mathjaxinline]A=\left[\begin{array}{cc} a & c\\ b& d \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline], then [mathjaxinline]T(\ell _1)[/mathjaxinline] contains the points [mathjaxinline]T(\left[\begin{array}{c} 0\\ b_1 \end{array} \right])=\left[\begin{array}{cc} a & c\\ b& d \end{array} \right]\left[\begin{array}{c} 0\\ b_1 \end{array} \right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} cb_1\\ db_1 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline], and [mathjaxinline]T(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1\\ m+b_1 \end{array} \right])=\left[\begin{array}{cc} a & c\\ b& d \end{array} \right]\left[\begin{array}{c} 1\\ m+b_1 \end{array} \right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} a+cm+cb_1\\ b+dm+db_1 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline]. Thus, [mathjaxinline]T(\ell _1)[/mathjaxinline] has slope [mathjaxinline]\frac{b+dm+db_1-db_1}{a+cm+cb_1-cb_1}=\frac{b+dm}{a+cm}[/mathjaxinline]. Similarly [mathjaxinline]T(\ell _2)[/mathjaxinline] contains the points [mathjaxinline]\left[\begin{array}{c} cb_2\\ db_2 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline] and [mathjaxinline]\left[\begin{array}{c} a+cm+cb_2\\ b+dm+db_2 \end{array} \right][/mathjaxinline], so [mathjaxinline]T(\ell _2)[/mathjaxinline] has slope [mathjaxinline]\frac{b+dm+db_1-db_1}{a+cm+cb_1-cb_1}=\frac{b+dm}{a+cm}[/mathjaxinline]. Therefore, we see that the image of the two lines have the same slope.
